Weaving of Cloth

Among all the tribes, the Atayal were most skilled at weaving hemp cloth. The fibers were twisted between the fingers, and no spinning wheel was used. Like the other tribes, the Taiya also used a seated backstrap loom. Weaving was considered women's work among the Atayal, and the quality of a woman's weaving determined her social status. The Atayal's skill at weaving hemp cloth was developed to a very high degree. Women in other tribes were also quite good at weaving.

 

 


Wood carving  

The Ami mostly carved utilitarian items, and seldom carved toys, relief sculptures, or the beams and pillars of their dwellings.		  

Ceramics		  

The Amis made mostly pottery jars. One feature of Ami pottery was the abundance of gourd-shaped and bottle-shaped vessels. Ami women were responsible for making pottery.


 

 



Wood carving  

The Bunun carved triangular notches and patterns of dots on their bows and the knife sheaths as a means of tallying the number of game animals killed or human heads taken.

Ceramics		  

The Bunun maintained traditions of pottery-making. They made pottery by stuffing clay into a round net bag, hollowing out the middle of the pot, and firing after the clay had dried. The surface of the finished pot had a net-shaped impression. These Bunun pots had a large mouth and lacked a base.

Wood carving  

The Yami were expert wood carvers, and a variety of geometric designs were carved on their canoes, the pillars and wall boards of their houses, the scabbards of their spoon-shaped knives, their fishhook boxes, and their ceremonial staffs. The Yami achieved a bright effect by painting their carvings with the colors red, black, and white. The Yami always do their best to carve fine and delicate patterns on their own valued large boats and houses.




Ceramics		  

The Yami made mostly pottery jars. One feature of Ami pottery was the abundance of gourd-shaped and bottle-shaped vessels. The Yami made ceramic bowls, dolls, and jars without legs. One notable characteristic of the Yami was there skill at making ceramic dolls.








Stone working  


Stone carvings with a strongly primitive flavour are often found in Paiwan settlements. The massive pillars of houses are carved with the images of male and female ancestors. Large pieces of slate, either alone or in rows, bear relief carvings of heads or ancestral heroes. The carved beams and columns of houses are often also crude yet forceful works of art.

Most aboriginal Stone Working took slate as a raw material. Besides the technical skill involved, in the Paiwan culture the use of stone has deep significance in terms of symbolism and the tribe's property system.




Wood carving

  


Paiwan wood carving was very highly developed.Although wood carving was done for ornamental purposes the tribes of northern and central Taiwan, the quantity was small and the level of artistic sophistication low.

In nature, the wood carvings produced by the tribes of southern Taiwan were either symbolic or realistic. Carving techniques included relief, intaglio, three-dimensional carving, and through-cut carving. A very wide range of objects were carved. Among the Paiwan, apart from toys and purely ornamental objects, buildings, furniture, tools, weapons, and religious implements were also decorated with carvings. The most common subjects of carvings among the Paiwan were human heads and pairs of snakes, followed by naked human beings, animal and snake designs, and water caltrop-shaped designs.







Weaving of Cloth
 


Among all the tribes, the Paiwan were very adept at embroidery.		  

Ceramics

  

Most Paiwan pottery vessels were in the shape of jars, but a small number were shaped like bottles or deeply-compressed bottles. Most decoration was applied to the mouth, neck, and upper half of the vessel. Decoration included raised patterns, impressions made with a hard object, and inscriptions made with a sharp point. Among the varied and attractive motifs were circles, triangles, rice patterns, interlocking chains, dots, and waves. Among the Paiwan, ceramic jars were considered hereditary treasures of the nobility.
